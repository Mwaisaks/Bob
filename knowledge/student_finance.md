# Student Finance — Practical Frameworks for Campus Life in Kenya

## The three money problems Kenyan university students actually face

### 1. Lump-sum income with flat expenses
HELB arrives once a semester. Mitumba sales arrive irregularly. Parent top-ups arrive when requested. But food, airtime, transport, and rent happen every week.

Without converting lump-sum income into a weekly budget, students almost always over-spend in the first two weeks and under-survive in the last two.

**Fix: The weekly envelope rule**
Divide any lump-sum receipt by the number of weeks remaining in the term. That amount — and only that amount — moves to your M-Pesa spending balance per week. The rest stays in Ziidi.

### 2. Invisible spending — death by a hundred small transactions
Lunch KES 80. Airtime KES 50. Chapati KES 20. Drinks at the union KES 120. Three weeks later you wonder where the money went.

None of these transactions feels like spending. None of them show up in a category that alarms you. But they add up to more than rent.

**Fix: The weekly tally rule**
Once a week, run your M-Pesa statement and add up everything under KES 200. That number will surprise you. Name it. Track it for four weeks. The awareness alone reduces it by 20–30%.

### 3. Fee bleed from fragmented transactions
Sending KES 200 to mum five separate times instead of KES 1,000 once costs KES 35 instead of KES 13. The money is the same. The cost is 2.7× higher.

Students with irregular income tend to send money as it arrives rather than batching. This is a habit that costs KES 200–500 per month in unnecessary fees.

**Fix: Batch sends once a week**
Collect all the people you need to pay. Send at the end of the week in single transactions per person. Use till numbers (buy goods, free) instead of cash where possible.

## Budgeting that actually works on campus

### The 50/30/20 rule (adapted for students)
- **50%** of any money received → fixed necessities (rent, food basics, transport)
- **30%** → flexible spending (eating out, entertainment, social)
- **20%** → savings buffer (Ziidi, M-Shwari, or a separate SIM)

For HELB recipients: apply this to the full disbursement before spending any of it.

### The runway number
Runway = current balance ÷ average daily spend

If your current M-Pesa balance is KES 1,800 and you spend an average of KES 200/day, your runway is 9 days. If next month's income arrives in 14 days, you are 5 days short without a plan.

Knowing your runway number shifts decision-making from "do I want this?" to "can I afford this and still make it to the end of the month?"

## Common money mistakes and their corrections

| Mistake | Why it happens | Correction |
|---|---|---|
| Clearing all debts the day HELB arrives | Social pressure, guilt | Negotiate deferred payments, stagger repayment |
| Fuliza for food | Balance hits zero before month end | Weekly envelope rule + Ziidi buffer |
| Sending money in many small amounts | Reactive — send as you get | Weekly batch send |
| Not tracking small purchases | No single transaction feels significant | Weekly tally rule |
| No savings because "I don't earn enough" | Waiting for the right amount | Start with KES 20/day — the habit matters more than the amount |
| Paying cash when a till is available | Habit | Buy goods tills charge KES 0 in fees |
